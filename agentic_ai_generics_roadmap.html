The Wedge, Decomposed

The seven workflows that make up patent and IP intelligence.

The brief says patent and IP intelligence — but that's a category, not a product. Inside it sit roughly seven concrete workflows. Concentrate on three for months 0–6, in this priority order. Resist the temptation to demo the rest. Every workflow on the homepage you can't deliver well costs you a reference.

Build · Months 0–6
I.

Orange Book patent landscape mapping

For any molecule a small generics shop is considering, produce a one-page landscape: every listed patent, expiry, type (composition, formulation, method-of-use, polymorph), Paragraph IV status, and 30-month-stay clock. Auto-refresh weekly from FDA Orange Book + USPTO PAIR. This is the demo you open every meeting with. Works on day one because the underlying data is public and structured.

Build First
II.

Paragraph IV opportunity scoring

The wedge inside the wedge. Score upcoming expiries by brand revenue, Orange Book patent count and type (formulation >> composition for challengeability), IPR institution history, current ANDA filer count, 180-day exclusivity availability, and manufacturing fit for a small shop. A single first-to-file decision is worth $10M–$100M. Today this runs on spreadsheets and gut. That gap is your wedge.

Sells the Deal
III.

Freedom-to-operate memo drafting (with citations)

When BD has shortlisted a molecule, the next deliverable is the FTO memo — claim-by-claim analysis of which patents stand between them and launch. Today: outsourced to IP counsel at $700–$1,200/hour or six weeks of internal time. An agentic system collapses this to a three-day reviewable draft, with every assertion linked to a specific claim, file wrapper entry, or prior-art reference. This is the workflow where "trust is the product" gets visibly engineered: per-assertion confidence scores, mandatory citations, refuse-to-answer floor, offline eval set.

Trust Layer
Defer · Months 6–18 expansion
IV.

Prior-art search for IPR petitions

Natural adjacency after FTO. Search prior art, map to claim elements, draft the IPR petition skeleton. Same buyer, bigger contract. Higher stakes — institution rate is roughly 62% for Orange Book-listed patents, so the bar for quality is real.

Phase 2
V.

Competitive ANDA intelligence

Who else is filing, where they are in their 30-month stay, settlement patterns, launch-at-risk history. Pulls from PACER (heavily concentrated in District of NJ and Delaware), FDA Paragraph IV list, Orange Book. Becomes a renewal-driver, not a wedge.

Phase 2
VI.

Suitability & citizen petition tracking

Early signals about regulatory blocking moves by brands. Niche but high-signal for portfolio strategy. Don't build until at least one customer asks twice.

Phase 2
VII.

Global IP intelligence — India, EU, Brazil, Mexico

Same molecule, multi-jurisdiction patent positions. Where the small NJ shops with parent companies in India become very interesting customers. Defer until the US workflows have two reference customers.

Week One

The data infrastructure to assemble before anything else.

This is where most of your six months disappears if you don't get it right. The list below is short on purpose — pick narrow, deep sources over a wide tooling sprawl.

FDA · Public
  • Orange Book (daily refresh)
  • Paragraph IV Patent Certifications list
  • Approved Drug Products database
  • FDA Drug Shortages
  • FOIA-released ANDA approval letters
USPTO · Public
  • PAIR / Patent Center — file wrappers
  • PTAB — IPR proceedings & decisions
  • Assignment records
Court · Public
  • PACER — Hatch-Waxman dockets
  • District of NJ & Delaware concentration is convenient
  • Federal Circuit appellate decisions
Commercial · Defer
  • IQVIA-equivalent sales data (use SSR Health / 340B proxies M0–M6)
  • DrugPatentWatch — benchmark, not dependency
  • GlobalData generics intelligence
The eval set the brief warns you about lives here. Assemble 100–200 historical questions where you already know the answer — the patent was litigated, the IPR was decided, the generic launched — and grade every model output against it before any prospect sees the system. This dataset is both the moat and the survival layer.
03 / 06
Org Chart

The buyer map inside a small generics shop.

For each target company, the org chart looks roughly the same. You are selling to one of three people, in this order of likelihood. The order matters — start in the wrong place and the deal dies in committee.

I.
Start Here · Budget Holder
VP / Senior Director — Business Development or Portfolio Strategy
Owns the "which molecules do we file next" decision. Has discretionary budget. Cares about Paragraph IV opportunity scoring and Orange Book landscapes. The dollar value of a single correct call dwarfs your annual contract — this is the buyer who can say yes without three signoffs.
II.
Second Meeting · Gatekeeper
Head of IP / In-House IP Counsel
Owns FTO and patent challenge work. Often the gatekeeper for legal-spend redirection. Cares about citation quality, audit trail, and whether the system will embarrass them in front of outside counsel. Bring them in after BD has validated, not before.
III.
Influencer · Not Budget
Head of Regulatory Affairs
Owns ANDA strategy. Important influencer, but not the budget holder for this wedge. Save for the Phase-2 conversation about ANDA Module 1 drafting and deficiency-letter response.
Avoid Quality and IT entirely in the first six months. They are where GxP procurement lives. The whole point of the wedge is to bypass them. If a conversation starts being routed to QA, the deal is already lost — go back to BD and re-pitch the workflow as a portfolio-strategy tool, not a quality system.

The Calendar

The six-month execution sequence.

Each milestone here is a forcing function. Miss the week-2 advisor papering and the whole thesis quietly collapses. Miss the week-8 first warm intro and you're running cold outreach into the hardest vertical on earth.

Weeks 1–2

Advisor & Targets locked

Advisor equity papered with milestone-based vesting (not just time). Three named Tier-A targets selected jointly with the advisor based on who they can actually open. Eval-set design started.

Weeks 3–6

Workflows 1 & 2 built — against real portfolios

Build the Orange Book landscape and Paragraph IV scoring tools against the three target companies' actual molecule portfolios. Do not build generic demos. Build the demos they will see.

Weeks 4–8

First five warm intros

Via the advisor. Discovery calls, not pitches. The output of each call is one specific molecule and one specific decision the buyer is making this quarter that your system would have changed.

Weeks 8–14

First design partner closed · $25K+ floor

Workflow 3 (FTO drafting) built specifically against their next filing decision. This is the contract that funds you and that you will show every subsequent prospect. Eval set hardened.

Weeks 14–22

Five more discovery calls · second/third design partner

Back half of Tier A. Second design partner closed. Eval set hardened to 300+ cases. First customer becomes a reference.

Weeks 22–26

Reference base · $80K–$150K ARR

Two-to-three named customers. Workflow 4 (IPR prior-art) scoped against the renewal conversation with customer #1. The wedge is now a beachhead — and the Phase-2 path opens.

Quiet Failure Modes

The two things that will kill this — beneath the brief's warnings.

The brief names hallucinations as the existential risk, and that is right. But two operational things sit underneath that, both worth naming explicitly — because both are silent until they're terminal.

Failure Mode · One

A single Paragraph IV miscall in a sales demo ends the campaign.

The NJ small-generics BD community is small enough that one "their tool told us patent X was invalidated when it was upheld on appeal" story ends every conversation in the market for a year. Buyers talk to each other. Your eval set is what prevents this. Treat the first demo with any real prospect as a launch event you only get to do once.

Failure Mode · Two

Advisor disengagement six weeks in.

The advisor relationship is the distribution channel and has its own failure mode — disengagement six weeks in because the role was structured too loosely. Equity vesting tied to specific introduction milestones (not just time), a standing weekly 30 minutes, and a named list of who they will open in month one — all need to be in writing before week three, or the brief's whole thesis quietly collapses while you're heads-down building.

· · ·

Build narrow. Sell to BD. Price at the floor. Demo on real molecules. Engineer the trust before the product. The wedge is patient — it rewards the founder who refuses to widen prematurely.
